1.3 Exploring the Robustness of the t Statistic

1.3.1 Introduction

Suppose one has two independent samples, \(x_1 , ..., x_m\) and \(y_1 , ..., y_n\) , and wishes to test the hypothesis that the mean of the \(x\) population is equal to the mean of the \(y\) population:

\[H_0: \mu_x=\mu_y\]

Let \(\bar X\) and \(\bar Y\) denote the sample means of the \(x\)s and \(y\)s and let \(s_x\) and \(s_y\) denote the respective standard deviations. The standard test of this hypothesis \(H_0\) is based on the \(t\) statistic

\[T=\frac{\bar X - \bar Y}{s_p \sqrt{1/m+1/n}}\]

where \(s_p\) is the pooled standard deviation

\[s_p=\sqrt{\frac{(m-1)s_x^2+(n-1)s_y^2}{m+n-2}}\]

Under the hypothesis \(H_0\) , the test statistic \(T\) has a \(t\) distribution with \(m+n‚àí2\) degrees of freedom when:

  • both the \(x\)s and \(y\)s are independent random samples from normal distributions

  • the standard deviations of the \(x\) and \(y\) populations, \(œÉ_x\) and \(œÉ_y\) , are equal

Suppose the level of signiÔ¨Åcance of the test is set at \(Œ±\). Then one will reject \(H\) when \[|T| ‚â• t_{n+m‚àí2,Œ±/2}\]

where \(t_{df,\alpha}\) is the \((1 ‚àí Œ±)\) quantile of a \(t\) random variable with \(df\) degrees of freedom.

If the underlying assumptions of *normal populations and equal variances hold, then the level of signiÔ¨Åcance of the t-test will be the stated level of \(\alpha\).

But, in practice, many people use the t statistic to compare two samples even when the underlying assumptions are in doubt. So an interesting problem is to investigate the robustness or sensitivity of this popular test statistic with respect to changes in the assumptions.

If the stated signiÔ¨Åcance level is \(\alpha = .10\) and the populations are skewed or have heavy tails, what will be the true signiÔ¨Åcance level?

If the assumption of equal variances is violated and there are signiÔ¨Åcant diÔ¨Äerences in the spreads of the two populations, what is the true signiÔ¨Åcance level?

1.3.2 Writin a Fuction to Compute the t Statistic

To begin, we generate some random data for the samples of \(x\)s and \(y\)s.

x=rnorm(10,mean=50,sd=10)
y=rnorm(10,mean=50,sd=10)

m=length(x)
n=length(y)

We compute the pooled standard deviation \(s_p\) and the the \(t\) statistic

sp=sqrt(((m-1)*sd(x)^2+(n-1)*sd(y)^2)/(m+n-2))
t.stat=(mean(x)-mean(y))/(sp*sqrt(1/m+1/n))

we can write a short R function \(t\) statistic to compute the t statistic.

source("tstatistic.R")
print(tstatistic)
function (x, y) 
{
    m = length(x)
    n = length(y)
    sp = sqrt(((m - 1) * sd(x)^2 + (n - 1) * sd(y)^2)/(m + n - 
        2))
    t.stat = (mean(x) - mean(y))/(sp * sqrt(1/m + 1/n))
    return(t.stat)
}

We try the function by placing some fake data

data.x=c(1,4,3,6,5)
data.y=c(5,4,7,6,10)

Programming a Monte Carlo Simulation

Suppose we are interested in learning about the true signiÔ¨Åcance level for the t statistic when the populations don‚Äôt follow the standard assumptions of normality and equal variances.

Given a particular choice of \(\alpha\), shape, spreads, and sample sizes, we wish to estimate the true signiÔ¨Åcance level given by

\[ \alpha^T = P (|T| ‚â• t_{n+m‚àí2, Œ±/2} ). \] Here is an outline of a simulation algorithm to compute \(\alpha^T\) :

  1. Simulate a random sample \(x_1 , ..., x_m\) from the Ô¨Årst population and \(y_1 , ..., y_n\) from the second population.

  2. Compute the t statistic \(T\) from the two samples.

  3. Decide if \(|T |\) exceeds the critical point and \(H_0\) is rejected.

One repeats steps 1‚Äì3 of the algorithm N times. One estimates the true signiÔ¨Åcance level by

\[\hat \alpha^T=\frac{\text{number of rejections of } H_0}{N}\] ### 1.3.4 The Behavior of the True Significance Level Under Different Assumptions

Suppose we Ô¨Åx the stated signiÔ¨Åcance level at \(Œ± = .10\) and keep the sample sizes at \(m = 10\) and \(n = 10\). We simulate samples from the following populations, where the only restriction is that the population means be equal:

alpha=.1; m=10; n=10
# sets alpha, m, n
N=10000
  • Normal populations with zero means and equal spreads (\(œÉx = œÉy = 1\))
# sets the number of simulations
n.reject=0
# counter of num. of rejections
for (i in 1:N)
{
x=rnorm(m,mean=0,sd=1)
# simulates xs from population 1
y=rnorm(n,mean=0,sd=1)
# simulates ys from population 2
t.stat=tstatistic(x,y)
# computes the t statistic
if (abs(t.stat)>qt(1-alpha/2,n+m-2))

# reject if |T| exceeds critical pt
}
true.sig.level=n.reject/N # est. is proportion of rejections

In this brief study, it appears that if the populations have equal spreads, then the true signiÔ¨Åcance level is approximately equal to the stated level for diÔ¨Äerent population shapes. If the populations have similar shapes and diÔ¨Äerent spreads, then the true signiÔ¨Åcance level can be slightly higher than 10%. If the populations have substantially diÔ¨Äerent shapes (such as normal and exponential) and unequal spreads, then the true signiÔ¨Åcance level can be substantially higher than the stated level.

Since the true signiÔ¨Åcance level in the last case is 50% higher than the stated level, one might be interested in seeing the exact sampling distribution of the t statistic.
